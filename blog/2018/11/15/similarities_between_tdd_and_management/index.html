Similarities between TDD and Management

The more I learn about management, the more similarities I find between management and software development. (which makes sense because they are just different ways to solve problems after all)

In today's post, I want to show you the similarities between Test-Driven Development (TDD) and meeting arrangement.

How to do TDD

Let's first see how we do TDD in the traditional Red/Green/Refactor cycle.

  1. Red

    In this phase, we write a test with detailed description to define the problem we need to solve.

    With this well written test, we define our goal for our next development cycle.

  2. Green

    Then we try to write minimal code to turn the test to green.

    We emphasize on minimal code because it's the only way to keep things simple. By keeping things simple, we let future requirements push us to more complexities1.

  3. Refactor

    After passing a test, we looked back to our code and test immediately and see if there's anything we can refactor.

With this process in my mind, I found that when we were unconsciously applying the same discipline to our management process, things work better.2

How to arrange a meeting

So, How do we arrange a meeting at Ekohe?

  1. Define the problem

    First, we try to define the problem we have right now.

    During this phase, we are not supposed to talk about any potential solutions.3 Because they would easily misguide the topic of our discussion and make people confused about why we are arranging this meeting.

    It's just like writing a test that's not coupled to our implementation. With the implementation in our mind, it's much harder to do so.

    I remembered once I proposed to do our daily stand-up over Slack (a potential solution), because I thought we were not communicating about our projects well enough (the problem). Then I got asked "Let's not talk about using Slack or not now. What's the real problem behind this?" After that we dug deeper together and found the real problem.

    In a word, the whole purpose of this phase is to set the goal for the meeting. Every meeting should have a clear goal (what problem this meeting needs to solve), otherwise, it's just a waste of time. (Or maybe your problem doesn't need a meeting to solve at all.)

    This goal is a test that can keep us focused and help us review our solution in a clearer way.

  2. Find a solution

    After the goal was set, we find the right person to join the meeting and try to find a solution together.

    We all know that meeting can be very time-consuming sometimes. Especially when we get the wrong group of people into a meeting, we are basically wasting a lot of valuable working hours by achieving nothing.

    So it's important to find the right person based on the goals we set.

    Is it a management problem?
    Find the PM.
    Is it a technical issue?
    Find the technical leader.
    Is it a spec misunderstanding?
    Talk to the client and find what they really want.

    Just like the green phase, we try to keep things simple, just solve the problem in our hand right now, and always defer complexities for later.

  3. Try to improve

    Finally, after the solution is deployed, we take a look back at the solution from time to time, and see if we can find ways to improve.

    We believe there is always more than one way to solve a problem.

    The only constant in this world is change. Everything around us is constantly changing.

    A solution that work today may not work tomorrow.
    That's why we need to look back from time to time and check if the solution is still working as expected.
    We will never know less than we do right now
    We'll always get more information and feedback for our problem/solution later. And with the new information, we can always improve our solution to fit the new context.

    So it's important for us to gather feedback and always try to improve (refactor).

They are almost the same

You may say there are still some differences between these two processes, like refactoring v.s. improvements. But the basic ideas and core principles are the same:

  1. Set clear goals at first
  2. Try to find the simplest solution
  3. Gather feedback and improve

No matter we are TDDing or arranging a meeting, we are both trying to solve a problem (technical or human-related). So it's natural that they share some similarities. And I believe there can be a meta way to follow here. Maybe I can also look back at this thought from time to time and see what it can be improved.
